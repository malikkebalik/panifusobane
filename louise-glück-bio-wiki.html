Who is Louise Glück?

Louise Glück is a famous American poet and essayist named Louise Gluck who has achieved many major literary awards, including the Pulitzer Prize, National Humanities Medal, National Book Reward, National Book Critics Circle reward, Bollingen Prize, and the Nobel Prize in writing.

Recently, in 2020, she was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature for her unmistakable poetic voice that with Nuti’s austere beauty makes individual existence universal.” She was the Poet Laureate of the United States from the year 2003 to 2004. The poet Craig Morgan Teicher has represented her as a writer for whom “words are always scarce, hard-won, and not to be wasted”.

Her poems hardly ever use rhythm, rather than relying on repetition, enjambment, and other techniques to achieve rhythm. Her poetry can be declared to be well-defined on trauma, as she has written all over her career about death, loss, rejection, the failure of relationships, and attempts at healing and renewal. She has pointed to the guidance of psychoanalysis in her work, as well as her early learning in ancient legends, parables, and mythology.

Louise has appreciated the influence of Leonie Adams and Stanley Kunitz. She is usually expressed as an autobiographical poet, her work is familiar for its emotional intensity and for normally drawing on myth, history, or nature to consider personal experiences and modern life. At the moment, she is an associate professor and Rosenkranz Writer in Residence at Yale University.

Louise Glück- Age, Parents, Ethnicity

Louise Gluck was born on 22nd April 1943, in New York City, USA. Her birth real name is Louise Elisabeth Gluck. Louise’s father is Daniel Gluck, a businessman, and Beatrice Gluck is her mother, a homemaker. Her mother was a degree holder at Wellesley College.

She is an American nationality and her ethnicity is Jewish, her paternal grandparents, Hungarian Jows resettled to the United States, where they finally bought a grocery store in New York.

In which school did Louise Glück attend?

As per her education, she received from her parents an education in Greek mythology and classic stories such as the life of Joan of Arc from an early age, she began to write poetry.

Later, she developed anorexia nervosa in which she described the illness, in one essay, as the result of an effort to assert her independence from her mother. She has connected her illness to the death of an older sister, an event that occurred before she was born.

She then attended George W. Hewlett High School, in Howlett, New York where she began psychoanalytic treatment.

She graduated in the year 1961 and also took a poetry class at Sarah Lawrence College, from 1963 to 1965, she went in for poetry workshops at Columbia University’s School of General Studies, which offered a degree program for non-traditional students, and studied with Leonie Adams and Stanley Kunitz whom she has acknowledged as critical coaches in her advancement as an artist.
